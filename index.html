2D Convolutions and Filtering.

Part 1.1: Finite Difference Operators

Using finite difference kernals Dx and Dy, we can achieve a gradient vector for the image. The magnitude of the gradients, sqrt(Dx(img)^2 + Dy(img)^2), serves as a way to detect edges. Binarizing the image takes the contrast to the limit. For this part, a good threshold for the binarized image turned out to be 0.25.

Part 2: Fun with Frequencies

Image sharpening, hybrids, and blending.

Part 2.1: Image Sharpening

Extracting the high frequencies of an image and re-adding it to the image creates a "sharpening" effect. We demonstrate this process on three images: the Taj Mahal, internet celebrity Zyzz, and my friend Sophia. For Sophia, we have a comparison of an original hi-res image and a sharpened version of blurred version of the image.

The differences are subtle but can be observed in high frequency regions of the images, such as the top of the Taj Mahal or by the ski rack.

Part 2.2: Hybrid Images

Adding the high frequencies of one image with the low frequencies of another creates a hybrid image: particularly, the higher frequencies will be more visible at a closer distance, and the low frequencies will be more visible from afar. The following gallery demonstrates this effect on images of several pairs of images.

Conclusion

One of the most important things I learned while working on this project is that it's good to make things easier for yourself. While coding, this means writing re-usable code as much as possible. However, it proved relevant several other times. For example, it was way easier to align images in photoshop than to use align_image_code. I also used photoshop to create the irregular mask. While it isn't in the spec of the project, it makes it significantly easier to create the results I want to see.

Additionally, I used HTML5 UP to generate this website, which made it a lot easier to format my HTML!
